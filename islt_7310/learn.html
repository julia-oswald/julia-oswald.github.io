Quadratics Introduction

Learning Goal 1:
Students can find and write the standard form and vertex form of a quadratic and identify key parts of the graph.


Quadratic functions have many real life applications which makes them a really fun type of function to study. In this section, we will be analyzing the graph of a function, a parabola, and examining different forms of the quadratic equation. We will first take a look at standard form of a quadratic and identify how to find the key features of the equation such as intercepts, roots, vertex, and the axis of symmetry. We will then take a look at the vertex form of a quadratic and identify the key features.

Polynomials

Learning Goal 2:
Students can describe and identify features of a polynomial graph and write a polynomial equation.


The lesson starts off with vocabulary necessary for completing problems of polynomials successfully. Some of these vocabulary word degree, coefficient, constant, leading term. Multiplicity is also introduced and covered in this section. Multiplicity is how a graph behaves at each zero, this is important for graphing polynomials. This section also talks about finding zeros or roots algebraically and writing a polynomial equation given the zeros of the function.

Finding Roots

Learning Goal 3:
Students can find all parts roots/zeros of a polynomial using either long division or synthetic division. Students will also be able to write an equation of a polynomial given itâ€™s roots.


This lesson will have two instructional videos that go along with it. The first video talks about dividing polynomials. Long division is first introduced followed by synthetic division. It is important to know how to do both methods since you cannot always use synthetic division. The second part of the lesson talks about finding zeros of polynomials after using some sort of division to break the equation into smaller parts. Our first big theorem of college algebra also appears in this second section, the fundamental theorem of algebra. Finally conjugate pairs are introduced when talking about rational and imaginary zeros of polynomials.
